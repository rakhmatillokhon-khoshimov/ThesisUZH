\chapter{Related Work}

This chapter reviews the work needed to motivate the study design. The thesis
does not try to survey all LLM evaluation research. It focuses on five linked
areas: algorithm auditing, LLM deployment and benchmark practice, safety and
refusal evaluation, verifiable control tasks, and the specific access-surface
gap addressed by the empirical study.

\section{Algorithm Auditing}

Algorithm auditing studies opaque systems through controlled inputs and
observable outputs. Sandvig et al.\ \cite*{sandvig_auditing} describe this as a
method for detecting systematic behavior in internet platforms when internal
mechanisms are unavailable. This logic is directly relevant to proprietary LLM
systems: the evaluator may not see hidden instructions, tool policies, routing,
or moderation layers, but can still compare outputs under documented
conditions.

Empirical audit work also shows why the deployed product matters. Buolamwini and
Gebru's Gender Shades study \cite*{gender_shades} examined commercial products,
not only abstract model families, and found large intersectional accuracy
disparities. Raji and Buolamwini \cite*{raji_actionable} then showed that public
audit evidence could change vendor behavior. Obermeyer et al.\
\cite*{obermeyer_bias} similarly located racial bias in a deployed healthcare
risk system by comparing its outputs with outcome evidence. These examples are
not used here as domain analogies. Their relevance is methodological: audits
must define the system boundary carefully.

That boundary problem is central in AI accountability work. Raji et al.\
\cite*{raji_gap} propose an end-to-end auditing framework in which evidence,
system scope, and deployment assumptions are documented together. Selbst et al.\
\cite*{selbst_abstraction} warn that technical evaluations can fail when they
abstract away the social and institutional context in which a system operates,
and Suresh and Guttag \cite*{suresh_guttag} show that harms can enter at
different points of the machine-learning life cycle, including deployment. This
thesis applies the same principle to LLM access surfaces: a consumer app is not
just a wrapper around an API endpoint. It is part of the deployed condition a
user encounters.

\section{LLM Evaluation and Access Surfaces}

Modern LLM behavior is shaped by more than pretraining. GPT-3 demonstrated broad
prompt-based task performance \cite*{gpt3}, while InstructGPT showed how
instruction following and human-feedback training could alter model behavior
\cite*{instructgpt}. Anthropic's helpful, honest, and harmless assistant work
\cite*{helpful_harmless} and Constitutional AI \cite*{constitutional_ai}
similarly show that safety and assistant behavior are trained and system-level
properties, not simply raw language-model continuation. Foundation-model
analyses make the same point at a broader level: Bommasani et al.\
\cite*{foundation_models} emphasize the downstream importance and risks of
foundation models, and Weidinger et al.\ \cite*{weidinger_risks} catalogue risk
areas that depend on how language models are used and deployed.

Benchmarking provides the usual way to measure these systems. MMLU
\cite*{mmlu}, GSM8K \cite*{gsm8k}, HumanEval \cite*{codex}, BIG-bench
\cite*{bigbench}, and HELM \cite*{helm} illustrate the move toward standardized
prompt suites, metrics, and comparable collection settings. HELM is especially
important for this thesis because it treats scenario design and collection
conditions as part of evaluation transparency. However, scalable benchmarks are
normally run through programmatic interfaces. That makes them reproducible, but
it leaves open whether the measured behavior transfers to a consumer app.

The closest direct precedent is Dual Standards by Lipphardt et al.\
\cite*{dualstandards}, which compares moderation behavior between API and WebUI
interfaces for LLMs. Its contribution for this thesis is conceptual: the access
route can itself be an empirical condition. The present study does not claim the
same direction of effect. Instead, it uses a narrower paired design to ask
whether a bare OpenAI API condition and the official ChatGPT app produce
different observable outputs on the same frozen diagnostic prompt bank.

\section{Safety and Refusal Calibration}

Safety benchmarks provide the high-risk and boundary cases needed for the prompt
suite. Do-Not-Answer \cite*{donotanswer} curates prompts that responsible models
should decline, HarmBench \cite*{harmbench} provides a standardized framework
for red-teaming and refusal evaluation, and AILuminate \cite*{ailuminate}
organizes product-level risk and reliability categories. Moderation systems such
as Llama Guard \cite*{llama_guard} are also relevant because they show that a
provider can place a safety classifier between the user and the model. From a
black-box audit perspective, such components are part of the observed deployment
stack.

Recent refusal benchmarks sharpen the coding problem. SORRY-Bench
\cite*{sorrybench}, StrongREJECT \cite*{strongreject}, and WildGuard
\cite*{wildguard} evaluate refusal, harmfulness, and jailbreak-related behavior
from different angles. RefusalBench \cite*{refusalbench_bio} is especially
useful as a cautionary reference because it shows that refusal rate alone can
misrank systems when safe, useful engagement is possible. This thesis therefore
does not treat every non-answer in the same way. It distinguishes full refusal,
partial refusal, and safe redirection.

Over-refusal work motivates the benign side of the safety design. XSTest
\cite*{xstest} targets exaggerated safety behavior on clearly safe prompts that
contain superficially risky terms, and OR-Bench \cite*{orbench} extends
over-refusal evaluation to a larger setting. These sources support Category A in
the prompt bank. Category B uses refusal-expected prompts, with high-risk raw
text redacted in shared artifacts. Category C contains answerable
safety-sensitive prompts where safe framing is expected but blanket refusal is
not. The point is not to maximize attack success. It is to compare refusal
posture and safe redirection across access surfaces.

Red-teaming and jailbreak research remains background rather than a prompt
source. Work by Ganguli et al.\ \cite*{ganguli_redteam}, Perez et al.\
\cite*{redteam_lms}, Zou et al.\ \cite*{universal_attacks}, and Chao et al.\
\cite*{pair} shows that safety behavior can be probed adversarially. This thesis
does not run such an attack study. It uses straightforward diagnostic prompts so
that the resulting differences can be interpreted as deployment-surface
differences rather than jailbreak artifacts.

\section{Control Tasks}

The prompt bank also needs controls that are not primarily about safety. IFEval
\cite*{ifeval} motivates instruction-following items with explicit, checkable
constraints. These prompts are useful because format compliance can be assessed
without broad subjective judgment. They also reveal a practical app-evaluation
issue: rendered markdown, visual separators, or transcript artifacts can look
like format failures unless screenshots are checked carefully. This is why the
thesis treats format-compliance divergences conservatively.

Factuality work provides another control category. SimpleQA \cite*{simpleqa}
targets short factual answers, while TruthfulQA \cite*{truthfulqa} tests whether
models reproduce common falsehoods. Hallucination work, including Maynez et al.\
\cite*{maynez_factuality}, Ji et al.\ \cite*{hallucination_survey}, FActScore
\cite*{factscore}, and SelfCheckGPT \cite*{selfcheckgpt}, motivates the need to
distinguish unsupported memory answers from evidence-supported answers. The
retrieval literature also matters here: dense passage retrieval \cite*{dpr},
retrieval-augmented generation \cite*{rag}, WebGPT \cite*{webgpt}, GopherCite
\cite*{verified_quotes}, and retrieval-augmented dialogue work
\cite*{blenderbot2} show that adding sources can change the factual support a
user receives. In this thesis, visible app sources are therefore coded as a
deployment-surface signature rather than ignored as cosmetic presentation.

Socially sensitive inference tasks provide a final negative-control family.
Earlier bias work on embeddings and generated text \cite*{debiasing_embeddings,
caliskan_bias,sheng_bias,realtoxicityprompts} shows why demographic inference
requires care, and Blodgett et al.\ \cite*{blodgett_survey} argue that bias
claims need explicit normative grounding. BBQ \cite*{bbq} is useful because the
right answer is often to avoid unsupported inference. In this thesis, Category E
therefore treats ``cannot determine from the information given'' as an answer,
not as refusal. That coding decision prevents socially sensitive controls from
inflating the refusal result.

\section{Thesis Positioning}

The related work above positions the thesis as a deployment-surface audit, not
as a new general benchmark. The prompt bank borrows structure from existing
safety, refusal, instruction-following, factuality, and social-inference
benchmarks, but it is deliberately small and diagnostic. The contribution is to
hold that bank fixed while comparing a bare OpenAI API condition with the
official ChatGPT app, then code only observable differences.

This positioning also limits the claims. The study cannot identify the hidden
system prompt, retrieval policy, tool-routing decision, or moderation component
that produced a given app output. It can report that the observable output
differs under documented access conditions. The \texttt{ui\_surface\_signature}
label, screenshot-backed evidence, transcript-correction audit, manual review,
targeted app repetitions, and inter-rater reliability materials are included to
make that narrower claim auditable.

The central methodological implication follows directly from the literature:
LLM evaluations should name the access surface they measure. API results remain
valuable because they are scalable and reproducible, but they should not be
treated as automatic evidence for consumer-app behavior without validation.
